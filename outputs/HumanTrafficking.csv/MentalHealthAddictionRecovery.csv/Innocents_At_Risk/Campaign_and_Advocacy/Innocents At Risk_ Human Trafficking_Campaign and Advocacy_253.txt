This legislation is important for preventing child exploitation, supporting survivors of trafficking, and holding big tech and social media companies accountable!Please find a summary below.On May 10, 2022, Senators Bob Menendez (D-N.J.) and Jim Risch (R-Idaho), joined by Senators Tim Kaine (D-Va.) and Marco Rubio (R-Fla.) introduced the International Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act of 2022 (S. 4171). • Reauthorizes the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000. • Expands prevention efforts at the USAID. • Instructs the United States Executive Director of each multilateral development bank to encourage the inclusion of a counter-trafficking strategy, including risk assessment and mitigation efforts in countries listed on the Tier 2 Watch List, Tier 3, and Special Cases in the Trafficking in Persons Report. • Expands prevention efforts at the USAID to counter traffickingOn March 30, 2022, Senators Amy Klobuchar (D-MN) and John Cornyn (R-TX) introduced the Abolish Trafficking Reauthorization Act of 2022 (S. 3946). • Reauthorizes the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2017.• Provides human trafficking prevention and assistance for human trafficking victims through education grants that prioritize local education agencies serving high-intensity child trafficking areas.• Provides processes to protect confidentiality and privacy of survivors. • Strengthens ability of state, local, and tribal child welfare agencies to identify and respond to children who are or are at risk of trafficking.On March 29, 2022, Senators Chuck Grassley (R-Iowa) and Dianne Feinstein (D-Calif) introduced the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act of 2022 (TVPA) (S. 3949).• Reauthorizes the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000. • Authorizes grants to enhance collaboration between state child welfare and juvenile justice systems. • Provides employment and education programs for trafficking victims with a history in the state child protection and welfare system. • Creates a pilot program for community-based organizations in rural communities to apply for funding to build replicable treatment models for housing models with support services for youth at high risk of being trafficked, such as those transitioning out of foster care or who have experienced the child welfare system. • Facilitates U.S. investigations into human trafficking cases. • Ensures anti-trafficking training and Codes of Conduct for all Federal departments and executive agencies. • Provides for a Government Accountability Office study to determine the accessibility of mental health care and substance abuse treatment for trafficking victims. • Modifies state plans for foster care and adoption assistance to require each state to maintain regular communication with law enforcement agencies and the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children (NCMEC), to ensure the safe recovery of missing or abducted children.On February 1, 2022, the Frederick Douglass Trafficking Victims Prevention and Protection and Reauthorization Act of 2021 (H.R. 6552), introduced by Reps. Chris Smith (R-N.J.) and Karen Bass (D-Calif.) on September 3, 2021, advanced to the House Committee on Foreign Affairs.• Reauthorizes the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000. • Provides human trafficking education grants to local educational agencies serving high-intensity human trafficking areas who partner with non-profit organizations specializing in prevention education, including law enforcement, technology and social media companies. • Implements the Human Trafficking Survivors Employment and Education Program to help trafficking victims achieve self-sufficiency and to prevent re-exploitation of trafficking through education, job-related skills training, life-skill training programs, and victim compensation. • Requires each governmental agency to book employees into commercial lodging that qualifies as a preferred place of accommodation, meaning that a zero-tolerance policy is in place concerning trafficking that includes employee training and reporting. • Expands age-appropriate trafficking prevention training in elementary and secondary schools and businesses. • Amends the Social Security Act to require states to maintain regular communication with law enforcement and the NCMEC concerning the safe recovery of a missing child by sharing information pertaining to the child’s recovery. • Reauthorizes and enhances International Megan’s Law to track convicted sex offenders living abroad who return to the U.S. • Expands trafficking prevention efforts at the United States Agency for International Development (USAID).On Monday, January 31st, 2022, U.S. Senators Lindsey Graham (R-South Carolina) and Richard Blumenthal (D-Connecticut) reintroduced the Earn It Act to encourage the tech industry to take online child sexual exploitation seriously.Removes immunity for social media and technology companies that knowingly facilitate the distribution of child sexual abuse material (CSAM) on their platforms.Updates existing federal statutes to use the term child sexual abuse material (CSAM) instead of child pornography. The term child pornography fails to describe the true nature of the images and undermines the seriousness of the abuse.Establishes a commission of survivors, technology representatives, and government stakeholders to create recommendations and voluntary best practices for tech companies to respond to the global pandemic of online sexual exploitation of children.Compiled by Jennifer Burton, Innocents at RiskNewer PostInnocents at Risk Talks Online Safety at Washington Global Public Charter SchoolOlder PostInnocents at Risk featured in new issue of Washington Life magazineChild trafficking is happening in every corner of the world, in major cities and small towns, and in every state and province. Children who have seen so little of life are taken by force or fraud and put into the worst of life: the child sex trade. Every 60 seconds, a child is trafficked into this dark and evil world. We all need to do everything we can to protect all children, to know what to look for and know how to report it.
